Lesson 2: The Noli Me Tangere

Introduction

In late 1884, Dr. Jose Rizal began writing a novel in Madrid and completed about half of it. He continued writing in Paris after finishing his studies and completed half of the second half. The remaining portion was written in Germany.

During a difficult time in December 1886, penniless and hopeless, Rizal almost burned the manuscript. Fortunately, a telegram from his friend Dr. Maximo Viola, announcing a visit to Berlin, renewed Rizal's hope. Eventually, Viola, who came from a wealthy family, helped with the funds needed to publish the Noli Me Tangere.

Symbolisms in the Noli's Cover

The cover of Noli Me Tangere has several symbolisms that represent the novel's themes and messages. Here are some of the symbolisms found in the book cover design:

  • Cross: sufferings.
  • Pomelo blossoms and laurel leaves: represent faith, honor, and fidelity.
  • Burning torch: the awakening of Filipinos to fight for their rights.
  • Silhouette of a Filipina: believed to be Maria Clara, Crisostomo Ibarra's lover, and symbolizes the plight of Filipino women during the Spanish colonial period.
  • Tombstone: religious faith
  • Chimera: a fantastic creature with the head of a Spanish-India mestiza and the legs of a Spanish friar, which represents the grotesque portrait of Rizal's patria adorada and evokes Philippine colonial society in general.
  • Feminine figure: represents all that is best in Philippine life, symbolizing constancy, courage, and religious faith.
  • Elevated and shadowed feminine figure: represents the feminine figure being placed in shadow despite being elevated, which symbolizes the oppression of women during the Spanish colonial period.
  • Religious orders: symbolize the religious orders running everything behind the scenes.
  • Death, cruelty, and bondage amid the lush tropical vegetation: symbolize the suffering and oppression of the Filipinos during the Spanish colonial period.

The Characters

Major characters who drive the story of Noli Me Tangere:

  • Crisóstomo Ibarra - Young Filipino reformer returned from Europe
  • María Clara - Ibarra's love interest, symbolizing ideal Filipino womanhood
  • Padre Damaso - Powerful, abusive Spanish friar
  • Padre Salvi - Meticulous and cunning man who uses his religious stature for political influence
  • Capitán Tiago - Wealthy Filipino merchant, María Clara's father
  • Pilosopo Tasyo - An old man who used to study philosophy and who prefers secular knowledge to Catholicism
  • Sisa - Poor mother driven mad by the loss of her sons
  • Crispin and Basilio - Sisa's sons, maltreated by priests
  • Don Rafael Ibarra - Crisóstomo's deceased father, unjustly persecuted
  • Doña Victorina - Pretentious Filipina who mocks her own culture
  • Don Tiburcio De Espadaña - Husband of Doña Victorina, who constantly abuses and dominates him
  • Doña Consolacion - A vulgar Filipina married to a Spanish officer; cruel and arrogant; imitates Spaniards and looks down on Filipinos
  • Captain General - The highest-ranking Spanish official in the Philippines
  • Elías - Mysterious man who helps Ibarra and fights injustice

Plot Summary

Crisostomo Ibarra returns to the Philippines after studying in Europe, hoping to improve his hometown by building a school. He is welcomed by friends and family, including his fiancée Maria Clara. However, he soon encounters corruption and oppression from the church and Spanish authorities, especially the friars Padre Damaso and Padre Salvi.

Ibarra learns that his father was wrongfully accused and died in prison. Despite his good intentions, his school project faces sabotage. After a series of conflicts—including a violent confrontation with Padre Damaso—Ibarra is branded a troublemaker.

Tragedy also unfolds for Sisa, a poor mother whose two sons suffer greatly, symbolizing the suffering of the Filipino people. Elias, a mysterious and brave youth, befriends Ibarra and tries to protect him.

Eventually, Ibarra is falsely accused of rebellion, arrested, and escapes with Elias’s help. Maria Clara, heartbroken, enters a convent. The story ends with Ibarra fleeing into exile, while the injustices and struggles of the characters highlight the need for change in society.

Themes and Symbols

Noli Me Tangere highlights the early social ills and injustices under Spanish colonial rule:

  • Colonial oppression and racial discrimination
  • Hypocrisy and abuses by the friars and government officials
  • The importance of education as a path to reform
  • Love, family, and Filipino identity
  • Symbols:
    • Maria Clara - embodiment of purity and colonial idealism
    • The sickly town of San Diego - symbolizing the nation's ailments
    • The school - representing hope for enlightenment and progress

Historical Context

The novel captures the Philippines in the late 1800s, emphasizing:

  • Spanish colonial dominance and its impact on Filipino society
  • Power and corruption of the religious orders
  • Emergence of the ilustrado class advocating reform
  • Growing nationalist sentiment for peaceful change

Impact and Legacy

Noli Me Tangere was a groundbreaking work that:

  • Exposed the abuses and injustices of colonial rule
  • Galvanized reformist and nationalist movements
  • Led to Rizal's persecution and eventual exile
  • Continues to be a fundamental piece of Philippine literature and history
