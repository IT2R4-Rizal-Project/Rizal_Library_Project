Lesson 3: Jose Rizal and Philippine Nationalism: National Symbol

Introduction

A Philippine national symbol will be considered official once it is declared through a law or a proclamation. The carabao, mango, and anahaw are widely known as national symbols, but they have no laws recognizing them as official national symbols. According to historical experts, Jose Rizal, who is widely considered a national hero, has not been officially declared a national hero in any existing Philippine law. On the other hand, a National Artist of the Philippines is a rank or title given to Filipino citizens in recognition of their contributions to Philippine arts and letters. Still, they are not considered national symbols representing traditions and ideals.

During the Commonwealth Era in 1934, Governor-General Frank Murphy declared sampaguita and narra as the national flower and national tree, respectively, through Proclamation No. 652. Philippine President Fidel Ramos declared the Philippine eagle as the national bird in 1995 through Proclamation No. 615, and the South Sea Pearl or Philippine Pearl as the national gem in 1996 through Proclamation No. 905. In 2009, President Gloria Macapagal-Arroyo declared arnis as the national sport and martial art through Republic Act 9850.

In February 2013, the Philippine Senate passed a bill declaring waling-waling as the national flower alongside sampaguita. A similar bill in the House of Representatives had already been passed in 2012. However, President Benigno Aquino III vetoed the bill, leaving the waling-waling as an unofficial national symbol.

Filipino National Symbols

  • No official declaration of national heroes:
    • - According to NHCP Section Chief Teodoro Atienza and historian Ambeth Ocampo, no Filipino historical figure has been officially declared a national hero through law or executive order.
  • Jose Rizal's status:
    • - Jose Rizal is mentioned as a national hero in the “whereas” clause of the Rizal Law (1956), but not in the main provisions of the law.
  • 1995 National Heroes Committee recommendation:
    • - The Technical Committee recommended nine historical figures to be declared national heroes.
    • - No official action was taken on this recommendation until it was revisited in the 14th Congress (2009).
  • Corazon Aquino's posthumous recognition efforts (2009):
    • - After her death, Congresswoman Liwayway Vinzons-Chato filed a resolution to declare Corazon Aquino a national hero.
    • - She later acknowledged that no Filipino has been officially declared a hero by law.
    • - On August 10, 2009, she urged Congress to recognize:
      • - The nine figures recommended in 1995
      • - Benigno Aquino Jr.
      • - Corazon Aquino
    • Congressman Salvador Escudero responded, saying heroes are shaped by people’s hearts, not legislation
    • The speech was referred to the Committee on Basic Education and Culture, but no further action was taken.
  • Philippine National Symbols Act of 2014 (House Bill 3926):
    • Filed by Rep. Rene Relampagos on February 17, 2014.
    • Aims to officially declare national symbols, including:
      • - Jose Rizal as the only historical Filipino national hero.
      • - Adobo as the national food.
      • - Jeepney as the national vehicle.
    • The bill's purpose:
      • - Promote nationalism and unity.
      • - Ensure respect and preservation of national symbols.

Rizal's Legacy in Modern Philippines

How Rizal continues to influence Philippine society:

  • What is the debate surrounding Rizal's status as a national hero?
    • Debate on Rizal's status as national hero:
      • - Ongoing debate on whether Dr. Jose Rizal truly deserves the title of national hero.
    • Argument 1 – Rizal did not lead a revolution:
      • - Critics argue that Rizal never led an armed resistance or revolution.
      • - National heroes should be those who fought and died for the country's freedom, like Andres Bonifacio and Katipunan leaders.
    • Argument 2 – Rizal was a "made-to-order" hero:
      • - Some claim Rizal was promoted by the American colonial government, especially by Governor Howard Taft (Ocampo, 1990).
      • - Americans favored Rizal because he did not oppose American colonization.
    • Argument 3 – Rizal supported American colonization:
      • - In writings like “Letter to the Women of Malolos” and “Filipinas Dentro de Cien Años”, Rizal appeared to:
        • - Have high hopes for American colonization.
        • - Believe it would bring freedom and progress to the Philippines.
  • Rizal’s Silence Does Not Mean Support
    • - Historian Ambeth R. Ocampo (1990) clarified that Rizal's silence on American colonialism does not imply support for it.
    • - Rizal did not speak against American rule because he died before the Americans fully established control in the Philippines.
    • - Rizal had no clear knowledge of how American rule would unfold.
    • - He was a pragmatist who believed in working within the system to achieve reforms.
    • - Rizal may have seen the Americans as potential allies against Spanish corruption and abuse, but not necessarily as legitimate colonizers.
    • - Ocampo emphasized that Rizal’s heroism was rooted in his excellence, integrity, and patriotism, not just his martyrdom.
    • - Rizal was a visionary who believed in the potential of the Philippines to become a free and independent nation.
  • In defense of Rizal as our foremost hero
    • Intellectual Brilliance:
      • - Rizal was a writer, philosopher, and revolutionary.
      • - His writings exposed Spanish abuses and inspired the Philippine Revolution.
      • - He awakened the national consciousness of Filipinos through his works.
    • Stand Against Colonial Oppression:
      • - Rizal resisted Spanish colonial rule.
      • - He became a symbol of peaceful resistance and inspired others to fight for the country.
      • - His name is deeply embedded in Filipino culture — seen in streets, schools, and institutions.
    • Role in the Propaganda Movement:
      • - Rizal was a key figure in this reformist campaign.
      • - His essays and letters promoted reform and national rights.
      • - His writings rallied Filipinos to seek justice and freedom.
    • Martyrdom:
      • - Executed on December 30, 1896, for his involvement in the revolution.
      • - His death became a powerful symbol of resistance and fueled the independence movement.
    • Influence on Filipino Identity:
      • - Rizal’s works helped shape Filipino culture and national identity.
      • - His influence is evident in literature, art, music, and patriotism.
      • - He inspired generations to value freedom, justice, and national pride.

Rizal's Symbolic Representations

Various ways Rizal is represented as a national symbol:

  • Values Derived From Rizal’s Life That Make Him Worthy To Be A National Symbol:
    • - Faith in God
      • - Maintained strong belief in God despite persecution and personal loss.
      • - Expressed trust in God’s will in letters and actions.
    • - Love of Parents
      • - Deeply loved and respected his parents.
      • - Studied ophthalmology to help his blind mother.
      • - Honored his father through art and literature.
    • - Perseverance
      • - Showed mental and moral strength in the face of hardship.
      • - Persistently worked for national reform despite adversity.
    • - Devotion to Truth
      • - Valued honesty and transparency.
      • - Wrote and researched to uncover and promote truth.
    • - Integrity
      • - Demonstrated strong moral principles and honesty.
      • - Lived as a model of upright character.
    • - Noble Thought and Conduct
      • - Advocated virtuous behavior and clear thinking.
      • - Encouraged parents to instill values in their children.
    • - Willpower
      • - Rejected colonial inferiority complex.
      • - Believed in Filipino potential and worked for national empowerment.
    • - Discipline and Self-Control
      • - Practiced reasoned decision-making over impulsive desires.
      • - Chose long-term good over immediate pleasures.
    • - Charity
      • - Willingly helped others, even at personal cost.
      • - Sacrificed his life as the ultimate act of love for the Filipino people.
    • - Patriotism
      • - Devoted to the Philippines’ freedom and progress.
      • - Promoted national unity and the full realization of Filipino identity.

Becoming a National Hero

Factors that contribute to Rizal's status as a national symbol:

  • Act No. 137 (1900)
    • The Philippine Commission named the combined districts of Morong and Manila as the “Province of Rizal” to honor Dr. Jose Rizal.
    • SThis marked the start of officially recognizing Filipino heroes.
  • Ways of Honoring Heroes
    • Celebrated through holidays, monuments, street names, public speeches, and other forms of commemoration.
  • Need for Clear Criteria
    • Due to the growing number of individuals celebrated as heroes, a standardized evaluation was proposed.
  • Formation of the National Heroes Committee (NHC)
    • Created under President Fidel V. Ramos via executive order on March 28, 1993.
    • Tasked with evaluating and recommending historical figures as national heroes.
  • Technical Committee Members (1993)
    • Included notable historians and scholars such as Drs. Onofre D. Corpuz, Samuel K. Tan, Alfredo Lagmay, Ambeth Ocampo (then Dom Ignacio Maria), and others.
  • Official Criteria for National Heroes (adopted June 3, 1993):
    • Must have a concept of nation and aspire/struggle for the nation’s freedom.
    • Must define and contribute to a system of freedom and order for the nation.
    • Must impact the quality of life and destiny of the nation.

Additional Criteria for Heroes

Adopted by the Technical Committee of the National Heroes Committee on November 15, 1995, in Manila:

  • Additional Criteria for Heroes (adopted November 15, 1995 by the National Heroes Committee):
    • A hero is part of the people’s expression.
    • A hero thinks of the future, especially of future generations.
    • Heroism is not based on a single event but on the entire process that shaped the person into a hero.
  • No Official Proclamation of National Heroes
    • No law, executive order, or proclamation has officially declared any Filipino historical figure as a national hero.
  • Honor Through Other Means
    • Despite the lack of official proclamations, laws and directives were issued to honor individuals for their significant roles in nation-building and contributions to history.
