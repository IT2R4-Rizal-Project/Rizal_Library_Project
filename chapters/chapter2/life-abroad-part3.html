Rizal's Life Abroad

The People Rizal Met During His Travels

IN SPAIN

  • Paterno Brothers - Antonio, Pedro and Maximo are Rizal's Friends who shared his ideas. They often held meeting and celebrations in their home.
  • Don Pablo Ortega Y Rey - A rich mestizo who was a former mayor of Manila. Befriended Rizal due to him being inlove with his daughter.
  • Consuelo Ortega Y Rey - One of the many lovers of Rizal. Very beautiful and educated and further described Rizal's poem dedicated to her.
  • Senor Roses - A bookstore owner whom Rizal frequently buys from.
  • Suzanne Jacoby - Another lover of Rizal while in Spain. Beautiful and pretty as described by him, confessed her love as he left Spain for France.
  • Marcelo H. Del Pilar - An illustrado and a leading figure of the propaganda movement. Founder of the famous La Solidaridad and a respected member of the Freemasons.
  • Mariano Ponce - Another famous figure in the propaganda movement. Also the co-founder of La Solidaridad together with Marcelo Del Pilar.

IN FRANCE

  • Dr. Niscase - A famous Parisian doctor. Rizal studied his works and also worked for him.
  • Maximo Viola - A very good friend of Rizal. The saviour of Noli Me Tangere because he helped Rizal when he became near destitute.
  • Dr. Louise De Weckert - A leading French ophthalmologist. Rizal worked for him as an assistant.
  • Paz Pardo De Tavera - A pretty girl and fiancée to his good friend, Juan Luna.
  • Nellie Boustead - A beautiful Parisian. The very girl who became the reason for the momentary rivalry between Rizal and Antonio Luna; the famous Revolutionary General.

IN GERMANY

  • Dr. Otto Becker - Another well known ophthalmologist. Rizal worked for him as an assistant.
  • Dr. Karl Ullmer - A kind protestant pastor. Rizal stayed at his home and became best of friends and became his admirer.
  • Prof. Frederich Ratzel - A famous German historian. Rizal befriended him.
  • Hans Meyer - A German anthropologist. He is a friend of Rizal.
  • Dr. Adolf B. Meyer - He is the Director of the Anthropological Museum. Also befriended by Rizal.
  • Dr. Feodor Jagor - A celebrated scientist-traveller. Author of the book: Travels in the Philippines which Rizal read as a child.
  • Dr. Hans Virchow - Introduced to Rizal by Dr. Jagor. He is a famous German anthropologist.
  • Dr. Ernest Schwelgger - A German ophthalmologist. Rizal worked for him too.
  • Madame Lucle Cerdole - Became Rizal's professor in French.

IN BOHEMIA

  • Prof. Blumentritt - A kind-hearted Austrian professor. Developed a lifelong friendship with Rizal.
  • Burgo Master - A kind town Mayor who welcomed Rizal in open arms.
  • Dr. Carlos Czepelak - A renowned scientist in Europe. He met Rizal at Leitmeritz.
  • Prof. Robert Klutschak - An eminent naturist. Also met Rizal in Leitmeritz.
  • Dr. Willkomm - A professor of natural history in the University of Prague.

IN PHILIPPINES(BACK)

  • Gov. Gen. Emilio Terrero - A liberal Gov. Gen. who defended Rizal from the friars because of the controversy caused by his novel; Noli Me Tangere and advised him to leave the Philippines.
  • Don Jose Luis Taviel de Andrade - A very good friend of Rizal. He became his lawyer at the same time his bodyguard.

IN HONG KONG

  • Jose Maria Basa - A Filipino resident in hong Kong. He also toured Rizal in Macau.
  • Balbino Mauricio - Another Filipino resident whom Rizal befriended while in Hong Kong.
  • Manuel Yriate - Also a Filipino resident in Hong Kong. He is the son of the alcalde mayor in Laguna.
  • Jose Slanz de Varanda - A Spaniard who served as a secretary of Gov. Gen. Terrero and became a spy by shadowing Rizal's movements in Hong Kong.
  • Josephine Bracken - Another well known lover of Rizal. She almost became her wife had he was not afraid of commitment.

IN MACAU

  • Don Juan Francisco Lecaros - A Filipino gentleman, who accommodated Rizal and Basa at his home.

IN LONDON

  • Dr. Antonio Ma. Regidor - A practicing lawyer where Rizal stayed at his home as a quest.
  • Dr. Reinhold Rozt - A librarian of the Ministry of Foreign Affairs. Recommended Rizal to the authorities of the British Museum and famously called him "a pearl of a man".

IN JAPAN

  • Seiko Usul - A lover of Rizal, very beautiful Japanese girl. She taught him Japanese language which he mastered within a week. Almost got Rizal to settle in Japan.
  • Tetcho Suehiro - A fighting Japanese journalist who was forced into exile by the government. Met Rizal on the Belgic and became friends.
  • Juan Perez Caballero - The secretary of the Spanish Legation in Hong Kong and invited Rizal to reside in the Spanish Legation.

Rizal's travels were not just journeys but experiences that shaped his vision for reform. His time aboard ships exposed him to different cultures, influenced his nationalist ideals, and ultimately led to his martyrdom.

José Rizal's Romantic Relationships

José Rizal, the Philippine national hero, had several romantic relationships throughout his life. These women played various roles in his personal and intellectual development. Here's a list of his most well-known lovers:

"Julia"

  • — While some sources mention a "Julia" as someone he courted as a teenager, others consistently identify Segunda Katigbak as his first love

Segunda Katigbak

  • — Rizal's "first love"or "puppy love"They met when Rizal was 16. She was a 14-year-old schoolgirl from Lipa, Batangas. Rizal met her when he was about 16. Their affection never progressed since she was already engaged to be married to another man.

Leonor Valenzuela

  • — The next lady to capture Rizal's heart was a woman named Leonor Valenzuela, a tall girl of regal bearing. She was Rizal's neighbor, regularly visiting her house during social gatherings. Rizal courted her with love notes written with invisible ink, which could only be read by heating the note over a candle. After Rizal left for Europe however, their romance ended and she accepted suitors and attended social parties.

Leonor Rivera

  • — Rizal's sweetheart for 11 years and his second cousin. They met when Rizal was 18 and Leonor was 13. A cousin of Rizal, Leonor was the inspiration and was believed that Rizal immortalized her for the character Maria Clara in Noli Me Tangere. Their relationship lasted for about 11 years, mostly through letters, but her mother disapproved of Rizal and kept his letters from reaching her. In 1890, Leonor informed Rizal she was engaged to Henry Kipping, an English railway engineer. The news of Leonor Rivera's marriage to Kipping devastated Rizal. Their relationship faced obstacles due to her mother's disapproval of Rizal and Paciano Rizal's disagreement with Rizal marrying before leaving for Europe.
  • Before Rizal returned to the Philippines on August 5, 1887, Leonor Rivera and her family had relocated to Dagupan, Pangasinan. To protect Rivera’s family from potential harm, Rizal’s father prohibited him from visiting her, as Rizal had already been branded a filibustero or subversive by the Spanish criollo elite because of his novel Noli Me Tángere. Rizal wanted to marry Rivera while he was still in the Philippines because she had been so faithful to him. Rizal asked permission from his father one more time before his second departure from the Philippines, but he never met her again.

Consuelo Ortiga y Rey

  • — Rizal met Consuelo, the daughter of Don Pablo Ortiga y Rey, while in Madrid. He wrote a poem for her titled "A La Señorita C.O. y R.", but he decided not to pursue the relationship to remain loyal to Leonor Rivera and partly out of respect for his friend Eduardo de Lete, who also admired her.

O Sei San (Seiko Usui)

  • — Rizal met Seiko Usui, also known as O-Sei-San, in Japan in 1888. She was daughter of a japanese samurai. Rizal met her when she was 23 years old, serving as his interpreter during his stay in Japan. She taught him Japanese art (su-mie) and language. She was educated and helped Rizal adapt to Japanese culture. When Rizal left for the United States, he told her in a note that he had spent a happy golden month with her and that he do not know if he can have another. Rizal considered settling in Japan because of her, but his patriotic mission led him to leave for Europe.

Gertrude Beckett

  • — A British woman Rizal met in London. she was the daughter of Rizal’s landlord, Charles Beckett. After his brief stays in Japan and the United States, Rizal chose to live in London, the capital of United Kingdom. The eldest of the three sisters, she was described as a curvy lady with cheerful blue eyes, brown hair, rosy cheeks, and thin lips.
  • Her affection for Rizal was clear, she spent more time assisting him than their other boarders and showered him with all her attention. ultimately though, Rizal backed out from the romance and decided to leave London for Paris abruptly in 1889 to be away from Gertrude so that she may forget him.

Nellie Boustead

  • — A wealthy Filipina of Spanish descent whom Rizal met in France. Nellie Boustead was the daughter of the wealthy Anglo-Filipino businessman Eduardo Boustead. The Boustead family welcomed Rizal in his stay in France. After learning of Leonor Rivera’s marriage to Henry Kipping, Rizal entertained the thoughts of courting another woman. The center of this idea would be the highly educated, cheerful, athletic, beautiful and morally upright Nellie.
  • One of the more memorable things about their romance was when Antonio Luna, who made rather distasteful comments about her, and Rizal almost had a duel to the death, only being stopped when Luna apologized for his comments, conceding Nellie to Rizal in a most chivalrous way.
  • Unfortunately, their relationship did not end in marriage as Rizal did not want to convert to Protestantism as Nellie demanded, and when the time Rizal had to leave had come, they parted as friends with no hard feelings.

Suzanne Jacoby

  • — A Belgian lady, Rizal met her when he stayed in Brussels as the cost of living in Paris was to expensive, he stayed in a boarding house managed by two Jacoby sisters, Suzanne and Marie. Rizal however did not seem to reciprocate the feelings as intensely as her. Rizal did not even mention her in his letters to his friends. When the time that Rizal had to (once again) leave, she wept, Rizal only leaving her with a box of chocolates.
  • Two months later, she wrote him a letter, saying "after your departure, I did not take the chocolate. The box is still intact as on the day of our parting. Don't delay too long writing us because I wear out the soles of my shoes for running to the mailbox to see if there is a letter from you. There will never be any home in which you are loved as in the Brussels, so, you little bad boy, hurry up and come back..."
  • Rizal went back to Brussels once again in April 1891, though not particularly to see her; instead, he focused on revising and completing the manuscript of El Filibusterismo.

Josephine Bracken

  • — The woman who lived with Rizal during his exile in Dapitan and was with him until his death.
  • In the final days of February 1895, while residing in Dapitan, Rizal encountered Josephine Bracken—an 18-year-old Irish girl with a petite frame, striking blue eyes, brown hair, and a cheerful personality. She was the adopted daughter of George Taufer from Hong Kong, who had traveled to Dapitan to consult Rizal for an eye condition.
  • Rizal felt a physical attraction toward her. Perhaps driven by loneliness and boredom, he found in her a welcome distraction—and once again, he fell in love. However, the Rizal sisters were suspicious of Josephine, believing she might be a friar's spy and viewing her as a potential danger to Rizal’s safety.
  • Taufer's double cataract was beyond Rizal's help, but he fell in love with Bracken. Taufer vehemently opposed the union but finally yielded. Bracken accompanied Taufer to Manila on his way back to Hong Kong, together with Rizal's sister, Narcisa, on March 14, 1895.
  • They attempted to get married, but due to Rizal's controversial reputation stemming from his writings and political views, the local priest, Father Obach, would only agree to officiate the ceremony if Rizal secured approval from the Bishop of Cebu. However, since Rizal declined to return to the Catholic faith, the bishop denied the request for a ecclesiastical marriage.
  • After accompanying her father to Manila and before returning to Dapitan to live with Rizal, Josephine took the opportunity to meet members of Rizal’s family in the city. Rizal’s mother proposed a civil marriage, considering it a lesser sacrament but a more acceptable option for Rizal’s conscience than making a political retraction just to obtain the bishop’s approval.
  • Rizal and Josephine lived together as husband and wife in a common-law relationship in Talisay, Dapitan. They had a son who, sadly, died just a few hours after birth. Rizal named the child Francisco Rizal y Bracken, in honor of his father.
  • She witnessed the events of the Tejeros Convention before heading back to Manila. Although summoned by the Governor-General, she was not subject to forced deportation due to her stepfather's American citizenship. Nevertheless, she chose to leave on her own and returned to Hong Kong. Eventually, she married a Filipino, Vicente Abad, a mestizo who worked as an agent for the Tabacalera company in the Philippines. A daughter, Dolores Abad y Bracken, was born to the couple on April 17, 1900. A later testimony of Abad affirms that her mother "was already suffering from tuberculosis of the larynx" at the time of the wedding. She passed away from tuberculosis in Hong Kong on March 15, 1902, and was laid to rest in the Happy Valley Cemetery. She was immortalized by Rizal in the final stanza of Mi Último Adiós, referring to her as “Farewell, sweet stranger, my friend, my joy…”
