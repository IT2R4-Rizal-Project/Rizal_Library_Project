Rizal's Life Abroad

First return to the Philippines

1887-1888

He had Four reasons for returning to the Philippines:

  • 1. To perform an operation on Dona Teodora’s eyes;
  • 2. To defend his oppressed countrymen more effectively than doing so in a foreign land;
  • 3. To find out how his Noli was received by the Filipinos and Spaniards; and
  • 4. To know the reason for Leonor Rivera’s long silence.
  • — July 3, 1887— Rizal left Marseilles (France), it was his first return Philippines after leaving it to study in Spain five years ago.
  • — August 6, 1887— he arrived in Manila and visited some friends.

At His Hometown:

  • — August 8, 1887– he reached Calamba. He opened a medical clinic and restored his mother’s vision.
  • — Newly arrived from Germany, he began to be known as “Doctor Uliman” (from the word Aleman) and soon he acquired a lucrative medical practice. He earned $900 from his services as a physician.
  • — Rizal opened a gymnasium for young folks, where he introduced European sports to discourage his townmates from idleness and gambling.
  • — He also took part in Calamba’s civic affairs and, on his spare time made paintings of the towns landscapes and translated the German poems of Von Wildernath into Tagalog.

The Controversy over the Noli

  • — Copies of the Noli Me Tangere had arrived at the Philippines weeks before Rizal’s return to the Philippines.
  • — The Spanish, particularly the friars, ended up with several of his book's copies. According to their assessment, the book was "heretic, impious, and scandalous in the religious order and anti-patriotic, subversive of public order, injurious to the government of Spain and its function in the Philippine Islands in the political order."
  • — The controversy over José Rizal's novel Noli Me Tangere reached Governor General Emilio Terrero. He summoned Rizal to Malacañang Palace to address the accusations against the book. Rizal defended Noli Me Tangere by stating it only exposed the reality of society.
  • — Out of curiosity, the Governor General read the novel and admitted enjoying it, finding no issue with its content. However, recognizing the danger to Rizal’s life, he assigned Jose Taviel de Andrade, a Spanish lieutenant, as Rizal's personal bodyguard.

Travels in Asia and the U.S.

First Trip to Hong Kong and Macao

  • — With a heavy heart, Rizal departed Manila for Hong Kong on February 3, 1888. He was not allowed to live in peace by his strong enemies. His parents' Calamba home received anonymous messages threatening his life.
  • — February 8, 1888— Rizal arrived Hong Kong and he was welcomed by Filipino residents.
  • — Jose Sainz de Varranda, Terrero’s former secretary, followed Rizal in the said British colony, and was believed to be commissioned by the Spanish authorities to spy on the hero.
  • — February 18-21, 1888— Rizal also visited Macao, a Portuguese colony near Hong Kong. he was invited to stay at the residence of Don Juan Francisco Lecaros, who was a former Filipino delegate to the Spanish Cortes.
  • — February 22, 1888— Rizal left Hong Kong, his destination was Japan.

Japan

  • — On February 28, 1888— Rizal arrived in Yokohama and later stayed at the Tokyo Hotel.
  • — Juan Perez Caballero— from the Spanish Legation, invited Rizal to stay at the legation to monitor his activities. Rizal accepted, believing he had nothing to hide.
  • — On March 7, 1888— Rizal moved to the Spanish Legation.
  • — Rizal admired Japan’s beauty, cleanliness, politeness, and culture, noting the charm of Japanese women and the lack of thieves and beggars compared to Manila.
  • — On April 13, 1888— Rizal left Japan for the United States.

Rizal’s visit to the United States (April-May 1888)

  • — April 28, 1888— Rizal arrived in San Francisco but faced quarantine due to a cholera scare. First-class passengers were cleared after seven days.
  • — May 4, 1888— Rizal stayed briefly in San Francisco, then traveled across the U.S., visiting Sacramento, Reno, Colorado, Chicago, Albany, and New York (May 13).
  • — May 16, 1888— Rizal departed New York for Liverpool, England.

Rizal’s Impressions of America

Good Impression
  • — the material progress of the country as shown in the great cities, huge farms, flourishing industries, and busy factories.
  • — the drive and energy of the American People.
  • — natural beauty of the land
  • — the high standard of living;
  • — the opportunities for better life offered to poor immigrants.

Rizal’s Impressions of America

Bad Impression
  • — the lack of racial equality: “America is the land par excellence of freedom but only for the whites.

London

  • — May 25, 1888 – mid-March 1889— Rizal stayed in London to improve his English and work on historical research to benefit Filipino reforms.
  • — May 1888 - March 1889 (London)— Rizal spent ten months in London studying at the British Museum and focusing on historical research for his book Sucesos de las Islas Filipinas. During this time, he wrote articles for La Solidaridad and defended Noli Me Tangere against criticisms.

Paris

  • — Rizal focused on revising and annotating Sucesos de las Islas Filipinas. He also wrote Por Telefono as a satirical response to critiques of Noli Me Tangere.
  • Language and Social Clubs:— Rizal perfected his French and organized the Kidlat Club to unite young Filipinos. This led to the foundation of a society aiming for Filipino recognition in intellectual fields.
  • Leisure and Social Life:— Rizal balanced his reform activities with socializing at homes of prominent Filipino families, including the Pardo de Taveras, Venturas, and Lunas.
  • — Despite a simple lifestyle, Rizal hosted a sumptuous Christmas dinner for friends before leaving for London.

Brussels (Capital of Belgium)

  • January 1890 (Paris to Brussels):— Rizal left Paris for Brussels, adopting a more frugal lifestyle while continuing his writings for La Solidaridad under pen names. (Dimas Alang and Laong Laan)

Notable Articles (1889 - 1890):

  • The Philippines Within a Century— Rizal’s critique of Spanish colonization.
  • The Indolence of the Filipinos— Defense of Filipinos against colonial stereotypes.
  • The New Orthography of the Tagalog Language— Introduction of new rules for Tagalog orthography.
  • — Rizal received letters about agrarian troubles in Calamba but, upon Paciano’s advice, decided to go to Madrid to support their case in court rather than returning home.
  • — Rizal informed Marcelo H. del Pilar of his plans to leave for Madrid to monitor the legal case regarding his family and other land disputes.

Misfortunes in Madrid (1890 - 1891)

  • August 1890 (Madrid)— Rizal sought help from Filipino groups and liberal Spanish newspapers to address the eviction of Calamba tenants but received no support. Many tenants, including his family, were forcibly evicted by Governor General Valeriano Weyler.
  • December 1890— Rizal received devastating news of Leonor Rivera’s engagement to Henry Kipping. This heartbreak coincided with his rivalry with Marcelo H. del Pilar for leadership in the Asociación Hispano-Filipina. Rizal declined the leadership to avoid division among Filipinos in Madrid and decided to leave the city.
  • Early 1891— Rizal took a vacation in Biarritz with the Boustead family, where he completed his second novel, El Filibusterismo.
  • March 30, 1891— Rizal left Biarritz for Paris by train and stayed at Valentin Ventura’s home.
  • April 1891— Rizal returned to Brussels to polish El Filibusterismo and decided to withdraw from the Propaganda Movement. He stopped writing for La Solidaridad to focus on his book and to encourage others to contribute.

Reasons for Leaving the Movement:

  • To focus on finishing El Filibusterismo.
  • To allow other Filipinos to take active roles.
  • To maintain unity in the Propaganda Movement under Marcelo H. del Pilar’s leadership.

Printing of El Filibusterismo:

  • July 1891— Rizal moved to Ghent for cheaper printing costs.
  • August 1891— The printing was halted due to financial issues, but Valentin Ventura provided assistance.
  • September 18, 1891— El Filibusterismo was published, and Rizal sent the first copy to Ventura in gratitude.

Back in Hong Kong

  • October 18, 1891— After publishing El Filibusterismo, Rizal sailed to Hong Kong to escape conflict with del Pilar, organize a propaganda movement, and reunite with his family.
  • November 20, 1891— Rizal arrived in Hong Kong, welcomed by Filipinos, especially Jose Ma. Basa.
  • December 7, 1891— Rizal wrote to his parents about returning home, discussing the unresolved agrarian problems in Calamba and the deportation of 25 Calamba residents, including his family members.
  • Christmas 1891— Rizal celebrated a joyful family reunion in Hong Kong.
  • Medical Practice— Rizal established a medical career in Hong Kong with Dr. Lorenzo P. Marquez, gaining a large clientele and helping the poor.
  • Relocation Plan— Rizal proposed relocating Filipino families to Borneo to escape Spanish oppression. He negotiated with the British but the project failed due to opposition from the Spanish government.
  • — Rizal proposed the Borneo colonization project to Governor General Eulogio Despujol but received no response. The Spanish consul-general in Hong Kong labeled the project unpatriotic, claiming it would cause labor shortages in the Philippines.
  • — Despite opposition from friends and relatives, Rizal decided to return to Manila to discuss the Borneo project, form La Liga Filipina, and address Eduardo de Lete's false accusations against him and his family.

Before his departure, he wrote three more letters—the first addressed to his parents and friends: the second one, to Filipinos; and the last Governor General Eulogio Despujol.

Despujol ordered his secretary, Luis de la Torre, to confirm whether the patriot had neutralized himself as a German citizen or not, and Rizal and his sister, Lucia, fell into the Spanish trap instead of receiving the safety he had sought. Without knowing what the Philippines would hold, the siblings set sail across the China Sea.
